*DESCRIPTION*:
This project focuses on performing large-scale data analysis on the NYC Taxi dataset using PySpark, with the primary objective of demonstrating scalable data processing and extracting meaningful insights into urban transportation patterns. The dataset consists of millions of records, making it suitable for big data analysis using distributed computing. PySpark was used as the core framework to efficiently process, transform, and analyze the data in a parallelized manner, ensuring performance and scalability.The analysis began with data exploration and preprocessing to ensure data quality and consistency. Missing values were identified across several columns, and appropriate strategies were applied to handle them based on the nature of the data. Columns with excessive missing values and low relevance were removed, while important fields such as passenger count and surcharge-related attributes were imputed using logical defaults. Data types were carefully corrected, especially for timestamp columns, to enable accurate time-based analysis. Duplicate records were also examined, and only meaningful cleaning steps were applied to avoid unintentional data loss.To enhance the dataset and enable deeper insights, feature engineering was performed. A new feature representing trip duration was created using pickup and drop-off timestamps, providing a clearer understanding of travel time. Additional temporal features such as pickup hour and day of the week were extracted to analyze ride patterns across different time intervals. Furthermore, trips were categorized into time segments such as morning, afternoon, evening, and night, allowing a more structured analysis of demand distribution throughout the day.The analysis revealed several important patterns. Time-based analysis showed that ride demand peaks during the afternoon and evening hours, with the highest activity observed around 6 PM, indicating strong commuting behavior, particularly during post-work hours. Demand was also found to increase towards the end of the week, with Thursday and Friday recording the highest number of trips, suggesting increased travel due to social and pre-weekend activities.Location-based analysis highlighted that a small number of pickup zones contribute to a disproportionately large share of total trips, indicating concentrated demand in specific high-traffic areas such as commercial centers or transit hubs. This insight reflects the uneven distribution of ride demand across the city and emphasizes the importance of these zones in urban mobility.Revenue analysis provided insights into customer payment behavior. It was observed that credit card transactions dominate both in terms of number of trips and average fare, while cash payments are less frequent and associated with lower-value rides. This suggests that users opting for digital payment methods tend to take longer or higher-cost trips, reflecting differences in customer preferences and trip characteristics.Overall, this project demonstrates how PySpark can be effectively used to handle and analyze large-scale datasets through distributed processing. By leveraging parallel computation and efficient data transformations, it is possible to uncover meaningful insights related to demand patterns, customer behavior, and revenue trends. These insights can be valuable for optimizing taxi operations, improving resource allocation, and supporting data-driven decision-making in urban transportation systems. These findings also highlight opportunities for future predictive modeling and demand forecasting.Further analysis could explore seasonal trends and external influencing factors.
